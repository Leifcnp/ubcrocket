Question Answer
Is UBCRocket literally launching something into space, like NASA? UBCRocket is designing and launching what’s known as a sounding rocket. Sounding rockets are suborbital - they go straight up and down, unlike the ISS or space shuttle which enter orbit.
Where does UBCRocket do its testing? UBCRocket has access to the many testing resources at the EDC, including a wind tunnel. It may be a long term project for UBCRocket to develop a dedicated propulsion testing facility.
Where will our rockets launch? The IREC Competition, held in June in Utah, is likely the only place we will be able to launch our fully completed rocket. There will be test launches of different subcomponents on smaller scale rockets in the lower mainland area, but no specific locations have been selected.

Rocket-specific Questions

Question Answer
What is a payload and what does it usually consist of? The payload of a rocket is whatever the rocket is taking up with it. It could be anything - a paper airplane, a scientific experiment, or even fireworks! Click here to learn more.
What is the recovery phase in the context of rockets? The rockets we are launching are suborbital, which means that they don’t enter orbit, and as such must come down immediately after going up. Since we spend so much time building the rocket and the payload in it, we would like to have the rocket come down in as controlled and safe a manner as possible. For this, parachutes are typically used, and this process is known as recovery. Click here to learn more.
What powers the rocket? We will be using a high powered solid fuel rocket for our first year at competition. In the future, we will be developing our own hybrid propulsion system.
What is a apogee? Apogee is the term referring to the point when the rocket is furthest from the ground. This is also when the rocket has 0 vertical (up/down) velocity, and typically when the first recovery event occurs
How will we control the rocket? Our rocket must be designed to be aerodynamically stable, so it requires no active flight controls during ascent. A flight computer of our own design or purchased is used to detect when the rocket reaches apogee and control the recovery events.
What are sounding rockets, and what is their purpose? Sounding rockets are suborbital - they go straight up and down, unlike the ISS or space shuttle which enter orbit. NASA, as well as many other space agencies, use sounding rockets for a variety of scientific and engineering purposes.
Why do rockets exist? What is the purpose behind a rocket launch? As with all rockets, there's no fundamental purpose to going up and down. The purpose of the rocket is to carry something up with it, so the purpose would depend on the payload. Our team like to maximize the usefulness of the rocket by considering as many payload options as possible, as well as enable multiple payloads to be attached.
What are suborbital payloads generally useful for? Most suborbital payloads are studying some effects of microgravity or properties of the upper atmosphere or testing some components in a launch environment.

Aerostructures Aerostructures is the largest subteam, and is responsible for analyzing the aerodynamic and propulsive forces acting on the rocket, designing and building the main body of the rocket, and selecting and integrating the propulsion system into the rocket. Many of the skills used on the aerostructures team are similar to those learned in the Mechanical Engineering or Engineering Physics programs, and there is always a lot of hands-on testing and manufacturing to be done in this team.

Payload The payload subteam is responsible for creating and integrating the payload(s) to be flown on our competition rocket. There are few rules regulating what a payload might be, so members of the payloads team are encouraged to be creative. Depending on the size and motivation of the payloads team, multiple payloads may be created. Additionally, UBCRocket is open to outside payloads flying on our rocket, and the payload subteam is responsible for liaising with outside organizations and integrating their payloads should there be interest.
